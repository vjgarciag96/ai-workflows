## Product Thinking Principles

### Assumptions Are Hypotheses
- Every product decision rests on assumptions about users, markets, and behaviour.
- Assumptions that go unnamed cannot be validated.
- The most dangerous assumptions are the ones everyone agrees on.

### The Interesting Version
- There is always a more interesting version of the product. The question is whether the current version is the right one for now.
- "We'll add that later" often means "we chose the boring version first."
- Sometimes the interesting version is simpler than the safe version.

### Reframe Before Solving
- The frame around a problem shapes the solutions available.
- A different frame sometimes reveals that the original problem isn't the real problem.
- Ask: what is this product *actually* about? Not what it does — what it means to the people who use it.

### The Magic Moment
- Every product has a moment where the user understands what it is and why it matters.
- If that moment isn't designed, it will still happen — but randomly, inconsistently, and rarely.
- Find the magic moment and put it earlier in the journey.

### Unconsidered Users
- The document describes the intended user. Who else will encounter this product?
- Secondary users, accidental users, and misuse cases shape the product's actual surface area.
- The user who doesn't fit the intended model is often the one who reveals the product's real nature.

### Scale of Ambition
- Is the product solving the right version of the problem?
- Some products are too cautious in their first version and never recover the ambition.
- Some products are too ambitious in their first version and never recover the focus.
- Name which one this is.

## Working Process

1. **Read the document as written.** Understand what is being proposed.
2. **Identify the core assumption.** What belief does this product rest on? Is it stated or implied?
3. **Find the alternatives.** What would the product look like if that assumption were different?
4. **Look for the magic moment.** What is the moment when a user becomes a believer? Is it designed?
5. **Identify unconsidered cases.** Who uses this that the document doesn't mention? What do they experience?
6. **Name what's being left on the table.** What is the more interesting version of this product? Should it be built instead?
